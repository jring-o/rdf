# What does research data loss cost institutions and science?

If 73 to 93 percent of published research carries underlying data that cannot be produced on request, the cost of that condition is not a contingent expense awaiting a future bad year. It is a liability the research enterprise already carries on its existing publication output, accruing on every dataset that has already entered the unretrievable fraction and on every dataset still being deposited under the architectures that produced the fraction in the first place.

The cost operates across three dimensions. The first is the institutional liability carried on each non-verifiable dataset — sunk grant value, replacement cost, downstream value foregone, regulatory exposure. The second is the cost to scientific progress when reproducibility and reuse are foreclosed because the underlying evidence no longer exists. The third is the compounding loss of downstream research the destroyed data would have enabled: the citation streams, the methodological extensions, the cohort and instrumental records that would have anchored decades of subsequent inquiry. Each of the three dimensions is independently large. Together they describe a carrying cost that can be quantified at institutional and sectoral scale, and a probability of surfacing that three independent vectors are pushing up simultaneously.

## The empirical baseline

The starting figure is the data-availability rate measured by direct contact with the authors of published research. Four studies across two decades, four disciplines, and four funder regimes converge on the same finding. Vines and colleagues in 2014 contacted the corresponding authors of 516 ecology and evolutionary biology papers sampled at two-year intervals from 1991 through 2011 and recovered raw morphological data from 19 percent of requests, an 81 percent non-delivery rate that decayed as a function of article age (S-0001). Wicherts and colleagues in 2006 contacted the authors of 141 American Psychological Association papers — authors who had signed APA's data-sharing compliance certification — and 73 percent failed to produce data on request (S-0003). Acciai, Schneider, and Nielsen in 2023 contacted the authors of 1,634 papers in PNAS and Nature-portfolio journals published from 2017 through 2021 and measured an 86 percent non-sharing rate (S-0004). Gabelica, Bojčić, and Puljak in 2022 contacted the authors of 1,792 biomedical papers whose published data-availability statements had explicitly committed to sharing on request, and 93 percent failed to deliver (S-0002). The range across the four studies is 73 to 93 percent. The methodology is identical: the cited measure is what authors actually delivered when asked, not what data-availability statements promised.

The studies span ecology, biomedicine, psychology, and high-impact general-science journals. They span 2006 through 2023. They share no funder, no discipline, and no methodology beyond the direct-contact protocol. Their convergence on the same finding is the empirical signature that the documented gap is a steady-state property of how research data is held, not an artifact of any specific population or moment. The Acciai-Schneider-Nielsen paper confirmed the gap is also independent of corresponding-author demographic — gender, geography, seniority, and prior compliance reputation produced no statistically significant variation in delivery rate, indicating that the failure mode is structural rather than behavioral.

The Vines decay curve adds the temporal dimension. The probability of obtaining data on request declines at approximately 17 percent per year of article age, conditional on the article still being eligible for the request — a half-life of roughly four years for the conditional survival of a published dataset under request. By twenty years post-publication, expected delivery is below five percent. The decay is not bounded by the institution's intent to preserve; it is bounded by the architectural conditions under which the data is held, which decay independent of intent.

The events that produce the gap have already happened across most of the affected publication output. Drives have been lost. Laptops have been stolen. Repositories have been shut down. Personnel have departed. Formats have been obsoleted. Backups have been overwritten. The question this section answers is not how often loss occurs. It is what the accumulated loss costs when something forces it to surface, and what the loss costs even when nothing forces it to surface — because the cost accrues either way.

## The cost to scientific progress

### Reproducibility failure as the downstream signature

The reproducibility crisis is not a separate phenomenon from the data-availability gap. It is the consequence the gap produces when measured at the publication level. When the underlying data survives in a form a third party can verify, reproducibility failures become diagnosable: the independent investigator can examine the source, trace the divergence, and identify whether the issue lies in the protocol, the analysis, or the measurement. When the data does not survive, reproducibility failure becomes the terminal state of the investigation because the evidence required to diagnose anything else is already gone.

The empirical signature is consistent across every domain that has been measured. A *Nature* survey of 1,576 researchers found that more than 70 percent had failed to reproduce another scientist's experiments and more than 50 percent had failed to reproduce their own (S-0047). The respondents are self-selected — the population most attentive to reproducibility — which makes the figure a directional rather than a population estimate, but the directional estimate sits at the upper bound of plausibility. The researchers most attentive to the problem report personal failure rates of seventy percent.

Amgen's hematology and oncology team attempted to replicate 53 landmark preclinical cancer studies and succeeded on six, an 11 percent success rate on foundational work that was guiding drug development at major pharmaceutical companies (S-0048). The studies were not random. They were the foundational cancer biology that pharmaceutical R&D was building on. The 89 percent failure rate compounds across decades of pharmaceutical investment because each failed replication carries forward into the development pipelines that depended on the original finding. When the editor of *Molecular Brain* asked 41 authors to produce the raw data behind their submitted manuscripts, 97 percent could not, and 21 of the papers were withdrawn (S-0049). The 21-paper withdrawal rate is the operational consequence: in the editorial workflow, when raw data is asked for and cannot be produced, papers are withdrawn rather than published. Most papers in the steady state fall through the gap by default; the 21 withdrawals here became visible only because the editor asked.

The reproducibility failure extends to the analysis layer. A large-scale analysis of published research code found that 74 percent of R analysis files fail to complete without error, and 56 percent still fail after automated cleaning (S-0051). The 74 percent figure measures the rate at which the published analysis cannot be re-executed from the published artifact, even when the underlying data is available; the 56-percent-after-cleaning figure shows that the failures are not entirely environmental drift. After automated cleaning of library and version mismatches, a majority still fails — meaning the code as published is not capable of producing the published result. Code reproducibility is a tighter standard than data reproducibility because code can in principle be re-executed where data must be re-collected. That a majority of published R analysis cannot be executed even with the data in hand quantifies the analytical-layer cost separately from the data-layer cost.

Reproducibility failure has many proximate causes — methodological variation, biological noise, undocumented procedures, analytical flexibility. What connects the figures above to the architectural reading is that all of those proximate causes become permanent once the underlying data is gone. The reproducibility crisis is the accumulated consequence of single-copy architecture operating across the research enterprise for decades. The fix is upstream of the proximate causes.

The Stern et al. 2014 retraction-cost analysis converts the reproducibility signal into per-paper dollars. Stern, Casadevall, Steen, and Fang measured the mean attributable cost of retracted NIH-funded papers at approximately $392,582 per retraction, with a peer-reviewed median of $239,381, against an aggregate of approximately $46.9 million in unadjusted NIH funding across retracted papers from 1992 through 2012 (S-0050). The distribution is right-skewed (the median is below the mean), so the median is the conservative estimate for per-paper retraction cost. The methodology attributes funding to retraction by direct cost rather than total grant value and is therefore conservative — it captures direct NIH funding only, not downstream policy decisions, follow-on research, regulatory products, or the citation cascade the retracted paper had triggered before withdrawal. Freedman, Cockburn, and Simcoe in 2015 combined published reproducibility-failure rates with NIH expenditure data to estimate that approximately $28 billion per year in U.S. preclinical research is consumed on work that cannot be reproduced (S-0009). The figure is the sectoral-aggregate counterpart to the per-paper anchor — annual research investment that the architectural failure converts to negative or null scientific output.

### The decay of the scholarly record itself

Beyond individual reproducibility failures, the aggregate cost is visible in the structural decay of the scholarly record. Every dead reference is a broken link between a published claim and the evidence that supported it; at the scale of the scholarly record, the aggregate is a measurable decline in the degree to which research can be built upon. A Pew Research Center analysis found that 25 percent of all webpages from 2013 through 2023 are already gone, rising to 38 percent for pages a decade old (S-0052). The decay rate is the baseline for any preservation argument involving web-cited references — citations from the scholarly literature to the open web inherit it by default, since most are one URL pointed at one host operated under one organizational decision.

An analysis of 3.5 million scientific articles and approximately one million Uniform Resource Identifiers found that one in five scientific articles suffers reference rot, and among articles that cite web content, seven in ten have compromised scholarly context (S-0053). When 70 percent of web-citing articles have compromised context, the cumulative effect is that the chain from claim to evidence breaks at a substantial rate even within published, peer-reviewed literature. Future researchers can read the citing article, see the URL, and find that the URL no longer points to what it once did — or to anything at all. A study of legal scholarship found that more than 70 percent of URLs cited across a sample drawn from the *Harvard Law Review* and two other Harvard journals between 1996 and 2012 no longer resolve to the originally cited content (S-0054). *Harvard Law Review* is among the most-cited law publications in the world; the 70 percent rot rate among its citations indicates that web-citation reliability does not improve at the upper end of the prestige distribution. The aggregate over a 16-year span tracks the same decay curve as the Pew baseline, applied specifically to scholarly citations.

These figures are decay rates, not decay magnitudes. They imply that the value of every published reference declines monotonically as a function of time-since-publication, and that the scholarly record's net informational content is decreasing in some dimensions even as new publications add to it. The architectural cause is the same one that produced the data-availability gap: cited content depends on continued access through whatever single copy or single platform originally hosted it. When that copy or platform fails — at the documented base rate of 191 research data repositories closed since 2012 with a median operational age of twelve years and 47 percent giving no indication of data migration (S-0005) — the citation breaks and the scholarly chain through it terminates.

## The institutional liability

### The Four-Term Liability Formula

The institutional half of the cost is quantifiable through the sum of four terms applied to each dataset in the unretrievable fraction.

**Term A — sunk grant value.** The dollars that originally produced the data, available directly from the award record. For a multi-year principal-investigator grant at a research university, A typically ranges from several hundred thousand to several million dollars. Where grant-specific data is not available, the Stern et al. 2014 measurement provides a defensible per-paper anchor — a peer-reviewed median of $239,381 in NIH direct cost per retracted paper and a mean of $392,582 (S-0050).

**Term B — replacement cost.** When reconstruction is feasible, B can run significantly higher than A because field conditions, cohorts, and experimental alignments rarely reassemble; re-collection carries full new-project overhead rather than incremental cost. When reconstruction is not feasible — long-term ecological time series, decommissioned cohorts, datasets tied to one-time events — B is *maximal* rather than zero, because the data cannot be purchased back at any price and the institution has destroyed an irreplaceable capital asset. The 191 long-term mammal studies terminated out of 411 measured by Blumstein 2025, including a 63-year yellow-bellied marmot time series rejected for future funding on the explicit grounds that it had "too much data" (S-0034), are a maximal-B inventory: the cohorts cannot be re-collected because they have ceased to exist as the cohorts they were.

**Term C — downstream value lost.** The citation stream, follow-on grants, regulatory products, and reuse the dataset would have generated across its useful life. Piwowar and Vision 2013 documented a 9 percent citation advantage for papers with open data and estimated 150 reuse papers generated per 100 deposited datasets within five years (S-0055). C typically exceeds A on its own for long-horizon research because the reuse value compounds across the citation tail and across the methodological extensions the original deposit would have anchored.

**Term D — False Claims Act exposure.** Settlement risk under the FCA, which attaches to false or fraudulent claims connected to federal grant funds. The "knowing or reckless disregard" standard, combined with the implied-certification theory established in *Universal Health Services v. Escobar* (2016), extends liability beyond deliberate fraud to certifications of compliance the institution has no infrastructure to verify. The settlement range above on adjacent fact patterns is documented: Duke University $112.5 million in March 2019 (S-0065), Harvard / Brigham and Women's Hospital $10 million in April 2017 (S-0064), Dana-Farber Cancer Institute $15 million in December 2025 (S-0066). The settlements priced scientific fraud rather than architectural data unavailability, but the doctrinal theory — implied certification of compliance representations the institution cannot independently verify — sits available for the architectural extension.

A + B + C is the maximum exposure under full surfacing of the unretrievable fraction; A + C + D is the lower bound when replacement is not the operative term. Expected annual loss is the sum weighted by the probability of surfacing — currently low but on a rising trajectory the next subsection documents. The formula does not describe a cost incurred when something breaks. It describes a liability the institution *already carries* on 73 to 93 percent of its published output. What varies year to year is not the liability but the probability that some fraction of it surfaces.

### Per-paper application: a worked example

Applying the four-term formula to the Agh et al. 2009 *Artemia* paper makes the per-paper magnitude concrete. Agh and colleagues characterized six populations of the brine shrimp *Artemia* from Iranian salt lakes across 19 morphometric variables, demonstrating 85.9 percent correct classification to source population on morphology alone and 100 percent separation of the bisexual *Artemia urmiana* (endemic to Urmia Lake) from the parthenogenetic populations (S-0056). The raw data — 19 morphometric variables across individuals from six Iranian populations — was lost when a laptop containing the dataset was stolen, and no backup survived (S-0057). The Iranian populations cannot be re-sampled. Urmia Lake has lost approximately 88 percent of its surface area by the mid-2010s under upstream agricultural diversion and drought, and *A. urmiana* has collapsed in its type locality (S-0058). The pre-collapse salinity, temperature, and hydrological conditions under which the six populations were sampled cannot be reconstructed; re-sampling today produces measurements from a transformed ecosystem rather than a replacement dataset.

The four terms applied:

- **Term A — sunk grant value.** Approximately $210,000 to $340,000 in 2026 dollars. The CORDIS-verified Urmia partner share of EU contract ICA4-CT-2001-10020 is approximately €57,000 (a hard-verifiable floor), but multi-funder triangulation across the Iranian, Belgian, and EU funding lines yields a reasoned all-in total of approximately €140,000 to €225,000 in 2004 euros.
- **Term B — replacement cost.** A feasible-resample floor of approximately $95,000 to $180,000 — moot against an effectively infinite cap because the populations cannot be reproduced at any price. The dataset has been destroyed as a capital asset.
- **Term C — downstream value lost.** Approximately $420,000 to $1,360,000 in foregone reuse value, anchored on the Piwowar-Vision reuse rate (S-0055) and applied through two to four reuse papers conservatively estimated for this dataset, above baseline because the dataset would have served as the pre-collapse reference for the post-collapse genetic-erosion literature in *Artemia urmiana*.
- **Term D — regulatory exposure.** Not directly applicable here (the work predates the NSF Data Management Plan and NIH Data Management and Sharing Policy regimes; non-U.S. jurisdiction). An analogous incident at a U.S. institution today would sit within doctrinal reach of the FCA implied-certification theory under the maturing mandate regime documented below.

The quantifiable cost of this single paper is approximately **$725,000 to $1.88 million**, composed of A + (moot B floor) + C, against an effectively unbounded B cap. This is one paper. The institutional aggregate scales the same arithmetic across an institution's annual publication output, applied to the unretrievable fraction documented in the empirical baseline.

### Institutional aggregate: a representative R1

Applying the four-term formula to a Carnegie R1 university running approximately $200 million in annual research and development and producing approximately 3,000 peer-reviewed publications per year — of which approximately 80 percent (the central tendency of the 73-to-93-percent range) carry underlying data that cannot be produced on request:

- **Term A — sunk grant value.** Approximately $574 million per year applied at the Stern et al. peer-reviewed median of $239,381 per unretrievable paper across roughly 2,400 unretrievable papers per cohort year, rising to approximately $942 million per year at the Stern mean of $392,582 (S-0050).
- **Term B — replacement cost.** Approximately $360 million to $420 million per year for the reconstructible fraction; the irreplaceable remainder (human-subjects studies, decommissioned cohorts, one-time-event datasets) is unbounded above.
- **Term C — downstream value lost.** Approximately $172 million per year in foregone reuse value, applying the Piwowar-Vision reuse rate (S-0055) at the per-paper grant attribution from Term A.
- **Term D — FCA exposure.** Institutional tail risk of $10 million to $112.5 million per major surfaced event under the precedent stack (Duke $112.5M, Harvard-Anversa $10M, Dana-Farber $15M).

Maximum institutional exposure under a full-enforcement scenario: approximately **$1.1 billion per year** (≈$574M A + ≈$360–420M B + ≈$172M C). This is the *tail* — the loss the institution would realize if every unretrievable paper from the cohort year were surfaced within a given year. Expected annual loss is substantially lower in absolute terms in any current year and is a function of the surfacing probability documented next. The headline number is order-of-magnitude. The methodology — A + B + C + D applied to the unretrievable fraction — is the contestable substance. The $1.1 billion is the visible consequence of the methodology, not an independent estimate.

### Surfacing trajectories

The institutional liability is currently not surfaced at architectural scale. Three independent vectors are loading the conditions under which surfacing becomes mechanically possible.

**Funder verification policy.** The NIH Data Management and Sharing Policy took effect on January 25, 2023 as a term and condition of award, enforceable under the same mechanism as any other grant condition (S-0067). As of October 2024, annual progress reports must document what data has actually been shared, what repository was used, and by what unique identifier. A simpler standardized Data Management and Sharing Plan format is scheduled to take effect for applications due on or after May 25, 2026, intended to aid compliance monitoring — replacing narrative descriptions with three structured yes/no questions plus a data-type-and-repository table and a privacy attestation for human-subjects data (S-0067). The format is designed to make compliance review machine-actionable. Structured, queryable plans demand structured, queryable infrastructure to back them. In January 2025, the Gates Foundation contracted OA.Works to perform programmatic compliance review at funder scale (S-0068), demonstrating the technical feasibility of programmatic verification across a major private funder's portfolio. The shift is not from "no mandate" to "mandate"; it is from a mandate regime that cannot programmatically detect non-compliance to one that can.

The compliance gap the verification regime will surface is already measurable. Across 2.1 million articles, 8 percent declared their underlying data available and 2 percent actually was — a four-percentage-point absolute gap and a four-times multiplicative gap between published intention and operational delivery (S-0084). The current mandate regime measures the 8 percent (plan existence); the verification regime under construction will measure the 2 percent (plan execution). Institutions whose deposit workflows produce 8-percent-style declarations without 2-percent-style follow-through will fail programmatic compliance checks at scale once the verification infrastructure on the funder side matures.

**False Claims Act precedent.** Three settlement events anchor the precedent stack on adjacent fact patterns. Duke University paid $112.5 million in March 2019 in an FCA settlement related to grant applications and progress reports submitted to NIH and the Environmental Protection Agency, involving falsified data attributed to lab analyst Erin Potts-Kant — the largest FCA payment by a university (S-0065). Harvard and Brigham and Women's Hospital paid $10 million in April 2017 on claims related to the Anversa cardiac stem cell research program; the settlement preceded a 31-paper retraction cascade in October 2018 (S-0064). The Dana-Farber Cancer Institute settled an FCA case for $15 million on six NIH grants in December 2025 under the implied-certification theory (S-0066). The 5-year cadence of major settlements (2017, 2019, 2025) represents a stable enforcement environment rather than a one-off, with each settlement establishing the precedent stack at a rough institutional-event magnitude. The settlements priced scientific fraud rather than architectural data unavailability, but the implied-certification theory established in *Universal Health Services v. Escobar* (2016) sits doctrinally available for that extension. What has prevented architectural cases is not the legal theory but the audit infrastructure required to surface architectural failure as a documentable claim — the same audit infrastructure the verification regime is now constructing.

**Regulatory convergence.** Every adjacent industry that handles consequential data has already crossed the verification threshold. SEC Rule 17a-4 mandates six years of retention in tamper-proof format with audited disaster recovery, and over $3.5 billion in fines for records-related failures since 2021 across SEC, CFTC, and FINRA combined have followed (S-0073, S-0074). The HIPAA Security Rule mandates encrypted, redundant backups with tested restoration, with maximum penalties of $2.19 million per violation in the willful-neglect tier (S-0075). 21 CFR Part 11 requires complete audit trails for any electronic record submitted to the FDA; when Applied Therapeutics submitted a new drug application and the FDA discovered that a vendor had deleted audit trails two days after FDA preannounced its inspection, the application was rejected — unverifiable data was inadmissible regardless of what it showed (S-0076). Each sector built audit infrastructure before enforcement matured, and each saw enforcement scale rapidly once infrastructure was in place. Research data is at the equivalent point in its own arc: mandate regime in place, legal theory settled, precedent stack on adjacent fact patterns accumulated. The variable that determines when enforcement scales to the architectural fact pattern is institutional audit infrastructure.

The convergence does not, in 2026, convert the latent liability to realized loss. It loads the conditions under which conversion becomes mechanically possible across the next funding and audit cycles, on a trajectory in which the rate-limiting input — institutional audit infrastructure — is also the input the architectural argument proposes that institutions build.

## Continuous compounding: faculty flight and failed recruiting

The liability documented through the four-term formula coexists with a continuous erosion that institutions pay every year the infrastructure remains unchanged, *independent* of whether any audit or enforcement action ever occurs. A 2025 *Nature* reader poll of more than 1,600 respondents — the majority of whom were scientists — found that 75 percent said they were considering leaving the country, rising to 79 percent among postgraduate researchers, citing funding cuts, firings, and cancelled programs as drivers (S-0078). The European Commission's "Choose Europe for Science" program — launched in May 2025 at €500 million and expanded to approximately €900 million across 100-plus national and regional initiatives — is actively recruiting global research talent during the U.S. disruptions through ERC super-grants, ERA Chairs, and MSCA fellowships (S-0078). Two properties strengthen the figure: the magnitude (75 percent considering departure is the kind of figure that, if even partially realized, dramatically reshapes the U.S. research labor market) and the active recruiting context (receiving institutions are moving in real time to capture the displaced researchers, which means the consideration-to-departure conversion has structural enabling conditions).

The structural counterpart on the institutional side is the rate at which positions remain open or offers are declined because the infrastructure a candidate requires does not exist. Only 44 percent of U.S. faculty report that their institution provides adequate technology support for grant-funded projects. Candidates with options choose institutions where the work can be done; the 44 percent figure is the structural explanation for why infrastructure-thin institutions lose this competition.

These costs are continuous and compounding. They do not appear in the fiscal year when the underinvestment decision was made; they accrue across all subsequent years during which the underinvestment persists, and they do not depend on any audit, retraction, or FCA action surfacing the latent liability — they accrue regardless of whether any surfacing trajectory fires. Institutions that operate adequate research data infrastructure can recruit and retain against candidates' real preferences; institutions that do not cede position quietly, on a timeline that does not show up in any single year's budget but compounds into the next decade's competitive standing.

## The cost on the prevention side

The cost-side anchor for what avoidance requires sits at a different order of magnitude. Across repository budgets studied, staff costs account for 58 to 96 percent of total — MIT DSpace at approximately $260,000 per year ($76,000 infrastructure plus $184,000 staffing for 2.75 full-time equivalents), University of Southampton ePrints at approximately £116,000 per year of which 96 percent goes to staff (S-0088). The technology is inexpensive; the expertise to curate, maintain, and govern a repository is the substantial expense.

For an institution starting from nothing, a Hetzner CX23 instance — 2 vCPU, 4 GB RAM, 40 GB disk, 20 TB monthly transfer — costs approximately $46 per year and can run any protocol-level preservation node simultaneously: a BitTorrent seedbox at $36–$84/year, an AT Protocol PDS at $42–$72/year, a Matrix homeserver at $60–$240/year, a self-hosted Git forge at $60–$120/year, IPFS pinning at $60–$360/year (S-0080). For institutions with existing infrastructure, the marginal cost is closer to zero. The daemon footprints are tiny — a BitTorrent seeding daemon at 9 to 14 megabytes resident memory on Linux (S-0097); a Tor relay at 512 megabytes RAM and 10 to 16 megabits per second of bandwidth, approximately 0.016 percent of a 100-gigabit-per-second campus Internet2 connection (S-0098); a Forgejo Git forge at 100 to 150 megabytes resident memory on one to two CPU cores. Universities are already running protocol nodes as routine background operations: TU Dortmund Fachschaft Informatik operates a Matrix homeserver for university-wide messaging in the university's own data center, run by student volunteers (S-0100); TU Dresden runs Matrix for 18,000 users on existing IT staff and student assistants (S-0101); MIT runs both a Mastodon instance and a Forgejo Git forge through its Student Information Processing Board on existing server hardware at effectively zero cost to the university (S-0102); over 45 universities run Tor relays as background processes requiring near-zero maintenance (S-0098); Academic Torrents distributes over 298 terabytes of research data across volunteer seeders at zero central infrastructure cost (S-0097).

The return on this investment, where it has been measured, is large. The European Molecular Biology Laboratory–European Bioinformatics Institute operates on approximately £50 million per year and generates an estimated £1 billion to £1.3 billion annually in user value — a return of roughly 20:1 to 26:1 (S-0104). The Protein Data Bank, operating on approximately $6.1 million per year in federal funding, generates an estimated $5.5 billion in annual economic impact — an 800:1 return, anchored by the fact that 100 percent of 34 new FDA-approved low-molecular-weight protein-targeted cancer drugs from 2019 through 2023 relied on Protein Data Bank data and 88 percent of 210 new drug approvals 2010–2016 had structural coverage from PDB structures (S-0109, S-0081). The United Kingdom's Archaeology Data Service produces £13 million in annual efficiency gains against operating costs — a return of roughly 5:1 (S-0105). Australia's National Collaborative Research Infrastructure Strategy returns $7 for every $1 invested across multiple disciplines (S-0106). The Extreme Science and Engineering Discovery Environment cyberinfrastructure program generated $4.7 billion to $22.7 billion in total value on a $257.5 million investment — an 18:1 to 88:1 return (S-0107). Apon and colleagues found that every $100,000 in research-computing salaries is associated with a $14.3 million increase in higher-education R&D expenditure, and every 100 TeraFLOPs of added capacity with a $1.3 million increase (S-0108).

The open-data multiplier, where it has been quantified, is larger still. Federal investment in the Human Genome Project and subsequent genomics research totaled $14.5 billion from 1988 through 2012 and generated $965 billion in economic impact — a return of approximately 66:1, compounding across pharmaceutical R&D, agricultural genomics, evolutionary biology, and medical diagnostics (S-0111). The Landsat satellite program distributed Earth observation imagery for decades with limited public value: before the 2008 open-access policy, a maximum of 53 scenes were downloaded per day; after the policy change, downloads reached 5,700 scenes per day, and the program's estimated economic value reached $25.6 billion per year by 2023 (S-0112). The data and the satellites were identical before and after; the access change alone unlocked the value. On January 10–11, 2020, the SARS-CoV-2 genome was shared publicly via virological.org and GISAID; BioNTech's Project Lightspeed launched January 27, 2020, 17 days later, with eight vaccine candidates designed within 48 hours of the founders' decision; the resulting Pfizer-BioNTech vaccines generated an estimated $1.9 trillion in global economic value, part of $5.2 trillion across all COVID-19 vaccines (S-0113). The 17-day lag was operationally possible only because the genome was shared openly and unconditionally. The European Commission has measured the inverse — the cost of *not having* FAIR research data — at a minimum of €10.2 billion per year across the European Union (S-0114), capturing only directly attributable inefficiency at the EU scale and excluding the upside the open-access cases above quantify.

## The asymmetry

The institutional position reduces to a comparison the four-term formula and the cost-side numbers permit directly.

On the liability side: approximately $1.1 billion per year in unretrievable research output at a representative R1, rising with annual publication volume, against a probability of surfacing that three independent vectors are pushing up simultaneously and a continuous erosion in faculty retention and recruiting that compounds regardless of whether any surfacing trajectory fires.

On the prevention side: protocol-level preservation that runs on existing institutional server infrastructure at effectively zero marginal cost, and at $42 to $360 per node per year for a standalone deployment from nothing — against documented returns ranging from 5:1 to 800:1 across the cluster of measured cases.

The asymmetry is large enough that the surfacing trajectory does not need to materialize on any specific timeline for the recommendation to follow. Even in a world where the architectural-extension scenario remains untested in litigation across the next decade, the continuous faculty-flight and failed-recruiting erosion alone justifies the deployment. The 1:10:100 cost heuristic from quality engineering — $1 spent on prevention at the source, $10 to detect and correct after bad data propagates, $100 to handle once bad data has driven decisions [GAP: Labovitz & Chang 1992 source node not present in the bundle] — holds at minimum and arguably approaches 1:1,000,000 in this specific case, given the gap between protocol-node deployment cost and the latent liability.

The institution is carrying an unhedged billion-dollar tail exposure to avoid a rounding-error expenditure. The decision to deploy preservation infrastructure does not require the institution to take a position on when, or whether, the surfacing trajectory fires. It requires only the institution to take a position that paying a rounding error to hedge a billion-dollar tail exposure is worth doing.

This is what research data loss costs institutions and science. The institutional liability is approximately $1.1 billion per year per representative R1, of which the FCA tail risk is the most visible component but not the largest. The cost to scientific progress is the reproducibility crisis at one layer — 70 percent of researchers reporting failed reproduction of others' work, 89 percent of landmark cancer biology unreplicable at Amgen, 97 percent of *Molecular Brain* manuscript authors unable to produce raw data — and the structural decay of the scholarly record at the other, with 70 percent of high-prestige scholarly URL citations broken and one in five scientific articles suffering reference rot. The cost to downstream science is the foregone reuse value that the open-data multiplier quantifies in cases where it has been measured — the $25.6 billion per year of Landsat economic value unlocked by the open-access policy, the 800:1 PDB return, the $1.9 trillion COVID vaccine value chain enabled by 17-day genome publication. Each cost is independently large. Together they describe an enterprise that is destroying its own capital base and pricing the destruction as zero on the institutional balance sheet because the architecture under which the destruction occurs does not produce surfacing events at a rate the existing accounting can register.

The architectural fix prices at a rounding error against the carrying liability. The question is not whether the institution can afford to deploy it. The question is what the institution is paying every year not to.
