## Individual-Level Resilience in Research Data Stewardship

The question of what individual researchers should adopt as standard laboratory practice [Q-0017] is not merely procedural—it is architectural. While institutional and funder-level coordination remains essential for systemic resilience, the researcher-level intervention proposed in **R6** [C-0045] offers a uniquely actionable and immediate countermeasure to data fragility. The recommendation is straightforward: maintain at least one local clone and one content-addressed copy of every research dataset, treating these as non-negotiable components of the research workflow.

The rationale for this practice is grounded in a stark operational distinction: reliance on centralized infrastructure versus the capture of its resilience properties. The 2019 GitHub-Iran episode [E-0009] illustrates this divide. Developers who maintained local clones retained full access to their repositories—every commit, branch, and line of code—despite jurisdictional restrictions. Those who depended solely on GitHub-hosted access faced permanent loss. A local clone of a Git repository is not merely a backup; it is a complete, cryptographically verifiable copy of the repository’s history. Its existence transforms a Tier 1 access restriction from a catastrophic failure into a temporary inconvenience [C-0045].

The implementation of R6 is deliberately low-cost. Commands like `git clone` and `ipfs add` are not capital projects but routine operations. For code, Git provides version control and integrity; for data, tools like BitTorrent, IPFS, or Git Large File Storage (LFS) enable content-addressed storage, ensuring that datasets are identified by their cryptographic hashes rather than their location. Where data is sensitive, Signal-style end-to-end encryption can be layered onto these systems without compromising their resilience properties. The cumulative effect of this practice across an institution is profound: every researcher’s local environment becomes an independent failure domain, collectively producing the **distributed redundancy** property [M-0002] at zero institutional cost.

Critically, R6 is the only recommendation in this regime that does not require institutional or funder coordination. It is implementable today, requiring only the researcher’s decision to integrate these commands into their standard workflow. While broader systemic changes (R1–R5) remain necessary, R6 demonstrates that individual action can meaningfully contribute to data resilience without waiting for top-down mandates. The operational triviality of the intervention belies its architectural significance: it shifts the default from fragility to redundancy, one laboratory at a time.
